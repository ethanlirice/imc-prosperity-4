# Hint 2
A profitable category is a starting point, not a conclusion. The correct follow-up question is why it performs as it does. Without that answer, you are holding a result without understanding the process that produced it.

Examine the products within your strongest clusters. Do they share measurable structural similarities? Do certain goods move together consistently, as if connected somehow? Do others diverge under specific conditions, creating predictable separation that can be anticipated and used?

Relationships between products are constraints. I treat all constraints the same way. I map them, test their stability, and calculate how they behave under varying conditions. A relationship that holds across multiple scenarios is a reliable input. A relationship that shifts is a variable that needs to be tracked rather than assumed.

Understanding isolated performance narrows the field. Understanding how products relate to one another sharpens every signal in that field. Confirmed relationships reduce risk. They strengthen conviction. They turn a reasonable position into a defensible one. Map the relationships, Captain. Then map which ones hold.

# Hint 3
Similarity does not imply simultaneity. That distinction is not semantic. It is structural. Two products can share behavioral characteristics while operating on entirely different timelines. The gap between them is a variable. Variables can be measured. Measured variables can be used.

Identify whether certain products consistently react before others. Does one category move first while another follows with a measurable delay? If so, map the gap. How large is it? How consistent? Does it hold across multiple rounds or does it compress and expand under different conditions?

A leading indicator is a forcing move. It commits the position before the response is visible. If you can identify which products lead and which lag, you are no longer waiting for confirmation. You are calculating the forced continuation before it arrives.
